Most product managers, if they're honest, would rank "support tooling" somewhere near the bottom of their dream product areas. It lacks the glamour of consumer apps, the scale of platform teams, and the prestige of revenue-driving features.

I think they're wrong. Here's why support tooling is one of the best training grounds for product craft.

## Real users, real problems — every single day

In support tooling, you don't hypothesize about user pain. You see it. Every ticket is a data point. Every frustrated agent workflow is a design failure you can fix. Every customer who can't find the right form field is screaming feedback at you through their behavior.

Most product teams would kill for this level of signal. We get it for free.

## Measurable impact within days, not quarters

Ship a classifier improvement on Monday, and by Friday you can see the deflection rate change. Redesign a form, and CSAT moves within a week. This tight feedback loop is *invaluable* for developing product instincts — you learn to trust your judgment because you can verify it rapidly.

Compare this to a consumer feature that might take 6 months to show statistically significant engagement changes. In support tooling, the feedback loop is compressed to days.

## The AI disruption wave is hitting here first

Every AI breakthrough in language understanding and generation has immediate applications in support:
- **Ticket classification** — Can we route this automatically?
- **Response drafting** — Can we suggest an answer to the agent?
- **Self-service** — Can the user solve this without ever submitting a ticket?

If you want to build intuition for AI product management, support tooling is the bleeding edge. You're not hypothesizing about AI use cases — you're shipping them, measuring them, and iterating daily.

## Enormous design challenges

Making a support form that feels obvious is *hard*. You're dealing with users who are already frustrated, who may not know the right terminology for their problem, and who need to be routed to the right team through their choices.

This is design at its most constrained and therefore most interesting. Every word matters. Every option you add is cognitive load. The best form is the one that feels like the system already understands your problem.

## The "two customers" dimension

In support tooling, you always have two users: the end-customer and the support agent. Optimizing for one often means trade-offs for the other. This dual-customer lens develops a sophistication in product thinking that single-user products simply can't teach.

---

If you're a PM early in your career, or thinking about your next move — don't dismiss support tooling. The best product instincts come from environments where the feedback is real, the impact is measurable, and the design challenges are genuinely hard.

Support tooling is all three at once.
